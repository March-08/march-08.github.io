**Introduction**\nIn Machine Learning we are often interested in knowing the relationships that exist between our data. Imagine you have a structured dataset, represented as a table in a DataFrame. You want to know if any columns are related to others, perhaps to know if there is any redundant information. It would also be useful to know if one of the variables is the cause of another. Let’s see how to do that in this article!\n## **Variance**\nLet’s now imagine that we have a dataset (a set of points) in one dimension. The first thing we want to do is to calculate the variance of these points.\n![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*FNOOX-zKmiYBNoUIBrMgSw.png)\nOne-Dimensional Data Points (Image By Author)\nThe variance intuitively tells us how far our points are from the mean. For example, if I eat one ice cream and you also eat one, on average we ate the same number of ice creams that is one. But even if I eat two ice creams and you eat zero, on average we always ate one ice cream each. It’s the variance that changes!\nIn our dataset, the mean is: *m = 1*\n![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*iq3koI6Vu0DiKCS2fboqtA.png)\nMean (Image By Author)\nTo compute the variance, we first calculate how far each point is from the mean.\n![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*-7kXVMw7ihVzXBC0lYIQ6Q.png)\nDistance from the Mean (Image By Author)\nNow we need to construct a square for each dashed line we have. I will only display the square for the first line, but you will have to do it for all of them.\n![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*z0fV-4p155CCyK6UPY_Dag.png)\nSquares for each distance (Image By Author)\nNow we simply need to **sum up the area of all the squares and divide by the number of squares-1**. Let’s say that the final variance is equal to *138*.\nThe formula we just applied visually is the variance formula.\n![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:536/1*INiuKnb-48v2_xRvnJvANw.png)\nVariance Formula (Image By Author)\n## **Two Dimensions Dataset**\nLet us continue by complicating things a bit but not too much. Let’s now imagine having a two-dimensional dataset. Then **each point is defined by a pair (x,y)**.\n![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*Vgjd1I4OTinyERZvBFKjIQ.png)\nBi-dimensional Dataset (Image By Author)\nWe already know that the variance of the point with respect to the x-axis is *138*. But now **we can also compute the variance of the y-axis**. It’s simple we can **project all the points on the y-axis and compute the variance in the same way as before**.\n![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*m5_QCoyRldBOCtkaq1t7jQ.png)\nProjection on the y-axis (Image By Author)\nThen, we found out that the variance with respect to the y-axis is (let’s say) *21*.\nNow **we have both var(x) and var(y)**. But **these 2 values alone don’t say too much about the distribution of the data in the 2 dimensions**.\nBy looking at the graph we can intuitively infer that there is a trend, while x increases, y also increases.\n![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*mdbtQYHmDNbnSVzr_hsMTQ.png)\nTrend (Image By Author)\nBut **it is not possible to infer this trend just by looking at the values var(x) and var(y)**.\nIn fact, **if we mirror the points**, (we multiply the x coordinate of each point by -1), **var(x) and var(y) will not change, but the trend will be the opposite**!\n![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*bdHf5TLlNNz82SUJCV8I3g.png)\nOpposite trend same variances (Image By Author)\n## **Covariance**\nHere comes the covariance between x and y. In order to compute the covariance, we have to **calculate the mean of the x coordinates and the y coordinates**. Let's say these means are 1 and 3. We can **plot 2 straight passing through these values**.\n![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*9x4pHV2uuEs_IK3gVW9PgA.png)\nStraights passing through the mean of x and y (Image By Author)\nNow we **calculate the area of the rectangles that these straight lines form with our points**. I will demonstrate this with a single point for simplicity.\n![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*RnHdMGenwsGP9S6Yppz6_w.png)\nCompute rectangles areas (Image By Author)\nTo calculate the area of these rectangles simply compute the difference between the point coordinates and the averages, and then multiply these differences between them.\n**Once you have calculated the area of all the rectangles simply add them up, and divide by the number of rectangles-1**.\nIn this way, we will have applied the covariance formula.\n![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:652/1*SbVRPFQokTlM3isKbw_gXA.png)\nCovariance Formula (Image By Author)\nNow that we know how to calculate the covariance, you can see that if the covariance is positive it means we have a positive trend, otherwise a negative trend.\nSo **covariance tells us how two variables are correlated, it does not, however, tell us how strong this correlation is**. In fact, the range of the covariance value is **\\[-inf, +inf\\]**, so it is not possible to define a *strength *of the relationship between the two variables.\n## **Correlation**\nCorrelation, on the other hand, is that value that lets us know **how strong the relationship between two variables is, it has a range of values in \\[-1, +1\\]**.\nThe formula for its calculation is as follows.\n![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:586/1*jOUzg6ak-1MpwABBti32rg.png)\nCorrelation Formula (Image By Author)\nSo if the values on the **x-axis grow and those in y also grow**, we would have a **correlation close to 1**.\nOn the other hand, if **x increases and y decreases**, we would have a **correlation close to -1**.\nIf the **correlation is zero, it means there is no correlation **between the two variables.\n## **The Causation Error**\nIt is often thought that in the case where there is a strong correlation, there is also a causal relationship. Sometimes this is true, in fact, we might have that variable x indicates how rich we are and variable y which indicates the size of our house. And we might infer that there is causation, i.e., the more money a person makes, the bigger his house is.\nBut often this is not the case. For example, suppose variable x indicates how many snow gloves we have, and variable y indicates how many soups we eat each week. Obviously, there is no causality between the two, though we notice in the data that the more snow gloves a person has, the more hot soups he or she eats each week.\nBut often what happens is that there is a** third variable that affects the two variables that we are not aware of**.\nIn this case, the third variable might be, how cold it is outside. So** the colder it gets the more snow gloves we have, and the more soups we eat. But gloves and soup do not have a causal relationship**!\n![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*C3PpbE2-z3Gg_UojDJSqGQ.png)\nCorrelation is not Causation (Image By Author)\n## **Final Thoughts**\nI hope this article has helped you to understand a little better these basic statistical concepts, which are very important in data science, though.
