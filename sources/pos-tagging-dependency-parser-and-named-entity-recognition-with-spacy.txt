dependency parser and named entity recognition with SpaCy\n### Mastering NLP with spaCy — Part 2\n### Introduction\nWords in a sentence provide a lot of information, such as what they mean in the real world, how they connect to other words, how they change the meaning of other words, and sometimes their true meaning can be ambiguous, and can even confuse humans!\n![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/0*w_G_I9Rrhh3lrXuM.jpg)\nImage from Unsplash Free\nAll of this must be figured out to build applications with **Natural Language Understanding** capabilities. Three main tasks help to capture different kinds of information from text:\n- Part-of-speech (POS) tagging\n- Dependency parsing\n- Named entity recognition\n### Part of Speech (POS) Tagging\n![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/0*GYAfTNyl60o3s7UG.png)\nIn POS tagging, we classify words under certain categories, based on their function in a sentence. For example we want to differentiate a noun from a verb. This can help us understand the meaning of some text.\nThe most common tags are the following.\n- **NOUN**: Names a person, place, thing, or idea (e.g., “dog”, “city”).\n- **VERB**: Describes an action, state, or occurrence (e.g., “run”, “is”).\n- **ADJ**: Modifies a noun to describe its quality, quantity, or extent (e.g., “big”, “happy”).\n- **ADV**: Modifies a verb, adjective, or other adverb, often indicating manner, time, or degree (e.g., “quickly”, “very”).\n- **PRON**: Replaces a noun or noun phrase (e.g., “he”, “they”).\n- **DET**: Introduces or specifies a noun (e.g., “the”, “a”).\n- **ADP**: Shows the relationship of a noun or pronoun to another word (e.g., “in”, “on”).\n- **NUM**: Represents a number or quantity (e.g., “one”, “fifty”).\n- **CONJ**: Connects words, phrases, or clauses (e.g., “and”, “but”).\n- **PRT**: A particle, often part of a verb phrase or preposition (e.g., “up” in “give up”).\n- **PUNCT**: Marks punctuation symbols (e.g., “.”, “,”).\n- **X**: Catch-all for other or unclear categories (e.g., foreign words, symbols).\nThese are called **Universal Tags**. Then each language can have more granular tags. For example, we can expand the “noun” tag to add the singular/plural information etc.\nIn spaCy, tags are represented with acronyms like “VBD”. If you are not sure what an acronym refers to, you can ask spaCy to explain with spacy.explain()\nLet’s see some examples.\n```plain text\nimport spacy\nspacy.explain(\"VBD\")\n\n>>> verb, past tense\n```\nLet’s try now to investigate the POS tags of an entire sentence\n```plain text\nnlp = spacy.load(\"en_core_web_sm\")\ndoc = nlp(\"I love Rome, it is the best city in the world!\"\n)\nfor token in doc:\n    print(f\"{token.text} --> {token.tag_}--> {spacy.explain(token.tag_)}\")\n```\n![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/0*_EPBEVja7tgJ7jSu.png)\nImage by Author\nThe tag of a word depends on the words nearby, their tags, and the word itself.\nPOS taggers are based on statistical models. We have mainly\n- Rule-Based Taggers: Use hand-crafted linguistic rules (e.g., “a word after ‘the’ is often a noun”).\n- Statistical Taggers: Use probabilistic models like Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) or Conditional Random Fields (CRFs) to predict tags based on word and tag sequences.\n- Neural Network Taggers: Use deep learning models like Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, or Transformers (e.g., BERT) to capture context and predict tags.\n### Dependency Parsing\nWith POS tagging, we are able to categorize the words in our document, but we don’t know what the relationships are among the words. This is exactly what dependency parsing does. This helps us understand the structure of a sentence.\nWe can think a dependency as a direct edge/link that goes from a parent word to a child, which defines the relationship between the two. This is why we use dependency trees to represent the structure of sentences. See the following image.\n![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/0*WuNnIvT2WjPw0Bmq.png)\nsrc: [https://spacy.io/usage/visualizers](https://spacy.io/usage/visualizers)\nIn a dependency relation, we always have a **parent**, also called the **head**, and a **dependent**, also called the **child.** In the phrase “red car”, car is the head and red is the child.\n![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/0*pnxpeeOsIpyaKOc5.png)\nImage by Author\nIn spaCy the relation is always assigned to the child and can be accessed with the attribute `token.dep_`\n```plain text\ndoc = nlp(\"red car\")\n\nfor token in doc:\n    print(f\"{token.text}, {token.dep_} \")\n\n>>> red, amod\n>>> car, ROOT\n```\nAs you can see in a sentence, the main word, usually a verb, in this case a noun, has the role of ROOT. From the root, we build our dependency tree.\nIt is important to know, also that a word can have multiple children but only one parent.\nSo in this case what does the *amod *relationship tells us?\n> The relation applies whether the meaning of the noun is modified in a compositional way (e.g.,\n\t*) or an idiomatic way (*\n\t*).*\nIndeed, the “red” is a word that modifies the word “car” by adding some information to it.\nI will list now the most fundamental relationship you can find in a dependency parsing and their meaning.\nFot a comprehensive list check this website: [https://universaldependencies.org/u/dep/index.html](https://universaldependencies.org/u/dep/index.html)\nWe can visualize the dependency tree in spaCy using the **display** module. Let’s see an example.\n```plain text\nfrom spacy import displacy\n\nsentence = \"A dependency parser analyzes the grammatical structure of a sentence.\"\n\nnlp = spacy.load(\"en_core_web_sm\")\ndoc = nlp(sentence)\n\ndisplacy.serve(doc, style=\"dep\")\n```\n![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/0*HbS0bR22I5y84b7Z.png)\nImage by Author\n### Named Entity Recognition (NER)\nA POS tag provides information about the role of a word in a sentence. When we perform NER we look for words that represent objects in the real world: a company name, a proper name, a location etc.\nWe refer to these words as **named entity**. See this example.\n![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/0*k9zYHcBSMJORyg2E.png)\nsrc: [https://spacy.io/usage/visualizers#ent](https://spacy.io/usage/visualizers#ent)\nIn the sentence “ *Rome is the capital of Italy* “, Rome and Italy are named entity, while capital it’s not because it is a generic noun.\nspaCy supports many named entities already, to visualise them:\n```plain text\nnlp.get_pipe(\"ner\").labels\n```\nNamed entity are accessible in spaCy with the `doc.ents` attribute\n```plain text\nsentence = \"A dependency parser analyzes the grammatical structure of a sentence.\"\n\nnlp = spacy.load(\"en_core_web_sm\")\ndoc = nlp(\"Rome is the bast city in Italy based on my Google search\")\n\ndoc.ents\n\n>>> (Rome, Italy, Google)\n```\nWe can also ask spaCy provide some explanation about the named entities.\n```plain text\ndoc[0], doc[0].ent_type_, spacy.explain(doc[0].ent_type_)\n\n>>> (Rome, 'GPE', 'Countries, cities, states')\n```\nAgain, we can rely on displacy to visualise the results of NER.\n```plain text\ndisplacy.serve(doc, style=\"ent\")\n```\n![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/0*QoDxN4QfUZika0xu.png)\nImage by Author\n### Final Thoughts\nUnderstanding how language is structured and how it works is key to building better tools that can handle text in meaningful ways. Techniques like part-of-speech tagging, dependency parsing, and named entity recognition help break down sentences so we can see how words function, how they connect, and what real-world things they refer to.\nThese methods give us a practical way to pull useful information out of text, things like identifying who did what to whom, or spotting names, dates, and places.
