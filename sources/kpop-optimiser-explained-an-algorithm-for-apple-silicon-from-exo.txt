X_data @ W.T + b\n    loss: Tensor = ((Y_pred - Y_target) ** 2).mean()\n\n    loss.backward()\n\n    grad_W: Tensor = W.grad.clone()\n    grad_b: Tensor = b.grad.clone()\n\n    with torch.no_grad():\n        dY: Tensor = 2 * (Y_pred - Y_target) / B  # BГ—R\n\n        W.grad.zero_()\n        b.grad.zero_()\n\n        kpop.step(grads=[grad_W], activations=[X_data], output_grads=[dY])\n\n        b -= 1e-2 * grad_b\n\n    if step % 50 == 0:\n        print(f\"Step {step}, Loss: {loss.item():.4f}\")\n\n```\n## Pros and Cons of KPOP\n### Memory overhead\nKPOP explicitly keeps and updates the following for each later,\n- $`\\bar R_i`$ в†’  the row-wise Kronecker factor (size RГ—R)\n- $`\\bar C_i`$ в†’ the column-wise Kronecker factor (size CГ—C)\n- $`\\bar Q_R, О›_R`$ в†’   eigenvectors and eigenvalues of $`\\bar R_i`$\n- $`\\bar Q_C, О›_C`$ в†’ eigenvectors and eigenvalues of $`\\bar C_i`$\n- $`m_i`$, $`v_i`$ в†’ AdamвЂ™s first and second moments  (both of size RГ—C)\n<empty-block/>\nwhich makes the memory requirements pro portional to $`O(R^2+C^2+RC)`$.\n- The $`R^2`$ and $`C^2`$ terms come from the KFAC/KFE factors.\n- The RC term comes from storing m and v, same as Adam.\nIn contrast:\n- Adam (or AdamW) only keeps m and v (both RГ—C) so its memory is proportional to $`O(RC)`$\nSo KPOP consumes more memory because it tracks the curvature SGD) may require many more steps (and thus more FLOPs) to converge, because it ignores curvature and might oscillate in steep directions.\n- KPOP effectively takes *better, more informed steps*, needing fewer of them to reach good solutions.\nEmpirically, this is why wall-clock time and step count to reach a target loss are lower for KPOP than Adam, despite higher cost per step.<br>\nThis Smarter Optimization with Curvature and Eigenvectors\n\n*For Curious Readers*\n\n## Introduction: Smarter Steps for Training Large Models\n\nWhen training a machine learning model, we want to find the parameters $\\theta$ that minimize the loss $L(\\theta)$.  \nWe adjust $\\theta$ step by step using the gradient:\n\n$$\\nabla_\\theta L = \\frac{\\partial L}{\\partial \\theta}$$\n\nand update:\n\n$$\\theta \\gets \\theta - \\eta \\nabla_\\theta L$$\n\nwhere $\\eta$ is the learning rate.\n\nThis is called **gradient descent**.  \nIt works well, but it assumes the loss surface is smooth and equally steep in all directions вЂ” which is rarely true.  \nIn reality, the loss surface is like a bumpy, twisted valley: steep in some directions, flat in others.  \nJust following the gradient leads to inefficient, zig-zagging paths.\n\n## Why Curvature Matters\n\nTo move more effectively, we need to understand the *curvature* of the surface вЂ” how steep or flat it is in different directions.  \nThis allows us to take big steps in flat directions and small steps in steep ones.\n\n**Think of it like driving:** If you're on a straight highway, you can floor the accelerator. But if you're navigating hairpin turns on a mountain road, you need to slow down in sharp curves and speed up on straightaways. The \"curvature\" tells you when to accelerate and when to brake.\n\nIn optimization, this curvature is mathematically described by the **Hessian matrix** вЂ” the matrix of second derivatives. But computing the Hessian directly is prohibitively expensive for large neural networks. \n\nInstead, we use the **Fisher Information Matrix**, which approximates the Hessian under certain assumptions:\n\n$$F = \\mathbb{E} \\big[ (\\nabla_\\theta \\log p(x; \\theta)) (\\nabla_\\theta \\log p(x; \\theta))^\\top \\big]$$\n\n**Why does this work?** The Fisher Information Matrix captures how much the model's predictions change when we slightly adjust the parameters. When predictions are sensitive to parameter changes (high curvature), the Fisher matrix has large values. When predictions are stable (low curvature), the Fisher matrix has small values.\n\nThe ideal update would adjust the gradient by the inverse of $F$, called the **natural gradient**:\n\n$$\\Delta \\theta = - \\eta F^{-1} \\nabla_\\theta L$$\n\nBut $F$ is huge and inverting it directly is impractical.\n\n## The KFAC Approximation\n\nThe first clever idea is to approximate $F$ efficiently using **Kronecker-Factored Approximate Curvature** (KFAC).  \nIt assumes some independence between layers of the model and approximates $F$ for each layer as a Kronecker product:\n\n$$F_i \\approx R_i \\otimes C_i$$\n\nwhere:\n\n- $C_i$ is the empirical **covariance matrix of the input activations** to layer $i$:  \n  it captures how different input features (the entries of the input vector $x^{(i)}$) vary together across the batch.  \n  It is computed as:\n  \n  $$C_i = \\frac{1}{B} \\sum_{b=1}^B (x_b^{(i)} - \\mu_i)(x_b^{(i)} - \\mu_i)^\\top$$\n  \n  where $x_b^{(i)}$ is the input activation vector to layer $i$ for sample $b$, and $\\mu_i = \\frac{1}{B} \\sum_{b=1}^B x_b^{(i)}$ is the sample mean.\n  In practice, the mean term is often omitted when activations are approximately zero-centered (e.g., due to batch normalization).\n  \n  Note that $x_b^{(i)}$ represents different things depending on the layer:\n  - For the first layer: the actual input features (pixels, tokens, etc.)\n  - For intermediate layers: the output activations from layer $i-1$\n  \n  If two input features tend to increase and decrease together in the data, $C_i$ reflects this by having a large positive off-diagonal entry.  \n  If they are independent, the corresponding entry is close to zero.\n\n- $R_i$ is the empirical **covariance matrix of the output gradients** (errors) at layer $i$:  \n  it captures how the components of the error signal (the gradient of the loss with respect to the layer's outputs) vary together across the batch.  \n  It is computed as:\n  \n  $$R_i = \\frac{1}{B} \\sum_{b=1}^B (y_b^{(i)} - \\nu_i)(y_b^{(i)} - \\nu_i)^\\top$$\n  \n  where $y_b^{(i)}$ is the output gradient vector (error signal) at layer $i$ for sample $b$, and $\\nu_i = \\frac{1}{B} \\sum_{b=1}^B y_b^{(i)}$ is the sample mean.\n  In practice, the mean term is often omitted when gradients are approximately zero-centered.\n  \n  Note that $y_b^{(i)}$ represents the gradients of the loss with respect to the outputs of layer $i$:\n  - These are the error signals flowing backward through the network\n  - They indicate how much each output dimension contributes to the total loss\n  \n  If two output gradients tend to rise or fall together (meaning errors at two outputs are correlated), $R_i$ reflects this correlation.\n\nWhy compute these covariances?  \nThe optimizer aims to approximate the curvature of the loss surface with respect to the weights $W_i$.  \nThe exact curvature involves the second derivative of the loss вЂ” or equivalently, the Fisher Information Matrix вЂ” which is expensive to compute.  \nHowever, under some assumptions, the Fisher block for $W_i$ can be approximated by the Kronecker product $R_i \\otimes C_i$.  \nThus, $R_i$ and $C_i$ summarize how the input signals and output errors co-vary, providing an efficient approximation to curvature.\n\n**But there's still a problem:** Even though KFAC makes the Fisher matrix easier to compute, $R_i$ and $C_i$ are still full matrices with off-diagonal entries. This means different gradient directions are still coupled together, making optimization complex.\n\n**The KPOP insight:** What if we could find a coordinate system where the curvature matrix becomes diagonal? In such a system, each direction would be completely independent, and we could optimize each one separately. This is exactly what eigendecomposition achieves!\n\n## Updating Kronecker Factors During Training\n\nSo far we've explained how to compute $R_i$ and $C_i$, but there's a crucial implementation detail: these matrices need to be updated continuously during training to track the changing curvature.\n\nKPOP uses **Exponential Moving Averages (EMA)** to adapt the Fisher information matrix as training progresses. Here's how it works:\n\n**Step 1: Compute batch-level estimates**\nAt each training iteration, compute fresh estimates from the current batch:\n\n$$C_i^{(t)} = \\frac{1}{B} X_i X_i^\\top \\quad \\text{and} \\quad R_i^{(t)} = \\frac{1}{B} \\Delta Y_i \\Delta Y_i^\\top$$\n\nwhere:\n- $X_i \\in \\mathbb{R}^{C \\times B}$ is the activation matrix for layer $i$\n- $\\Delta Y_i \\in \\mathbb{R}^{R \\times B}$ is the gradient matrix (error signals) for layer $i$\n- $B$ is the batch size\n- $t$ denotes the current training iteration\n\n**Step 2: Update EMA estimates**\nInstead of using the raw batch estimates, maintain running averages:\n\n$$\\tilde{C}_i^{(t)} \\leftarrow \\alpha \\tilde{C}_i^{(t-1)} + (1-\\alpha) C_i^{(t)}$$\n\n$$\\tilde{R}_i^{(t)} \\leftarrow \\alpha \\tilde{R}_i^{(t-1)} + (1-\\alpha) R_i^{(t)}$$\n\nwhere $\\alpha \\in [0,1)$ is the decay parameter (typically $\\alpha = 0.95$ or $\\alpha = 0.99$).\n\n**Why use EMA instead of raw batch estimates?**\n- **Stability**: Raw batch estimates are noisy; EMA provides smooth, stable curvature estimates\n- **Memory of past geometry**: The curvature information from previous iterations helps guide current updates\n- **Robustness**: Reduces sensitivity to outlier batches or sudden changes in data distribution\n\n**The eigendecomposition step:**\nThe eigenvectors and eigenvalues are computed from the EMA estimates $\\tilde{R}_i$ and $\\tilde{C}_i$, not the raw batch estimates:\n\n$$\\tilde{R}_i = Q_R \\Lambda_R Q_R^\\top, \\quad \\tilde{C}_i = Q_C \\Lambda_C Q_C^\\top$$\n\nThis EMA mechanism is what makes KPOP practical for real training scenarios!\n\n## Why Eigenvectors and Eigenvalues Help\n\nEven with KFAC, $R_i$ and $C_i$ are still full matrices вЂ” so different directions of the gradient still interact.  \nTo make each direction independent, we diagonalize $R_i$ and $C_i$ by computing their **eigenvalues** and **eigenvectors**:\n\n$$R_i = Q_R \\Lambda_R Q_R^\\top, \\quad C_i = Q_C \\Lambda_C Q_C^\\top$$\n\nwhere:\n- $Q_R$, $Q_C$ are matrices whose columns are eigenvectors вЂ” special directions in which the matrix acts by scaling only.\n- $\\Lambda_R$, $\\Lambda_C$ are diagonal matrices of eigenvalues вЂ” the amount of scaling in each eigenvector direction.\n\n**Why is this crucial?** Think of it this way: if you're hiking in mountains, you want to know which directions are steep cliffs (high eigenvalues) versus gentle slopes (low eigenvalues). The eigenvectors point you toward these natural directions of the landscape, while eigenvalues tell you how steep each direction is.\n\nIn this eigenbasis, the curvature becomes diagonal вЂ” each direction is independent and we know exactly how steep it is (from the eigenvalues). This transforms a complex, intertwined optimization problem into a collection of simple, independent 1D problems.\n\n## Transforming the Gradient\n\nHere is the core idea of KPOP:\n\n1. Compute the gradient of the loss with respect to the weights at layer $i$, denoted $G_i$.\n\n2. Rotate $G_i$ into the eigenbasis:\n   \n   $$G'_i = Q_R^\\top G_i Q_C$$\n   \n   This expresses the gradient in terms of the eigenvectors of $R_i$ and $C_i$.\n   \n   In this rotated space:\n   - The curvature is diagonal.\n   - Each direction (each entry in $G'_i$) corresponds to an independent direction of steepest descent.\n\n3. Apply an adaptive optimizer (like Adam) to $G'_i$, adjusting the step in each direction according to its eigenvalue and past gradients:\n   \n   $$m_t = \\beta_1 m_{t-1} + (1-\\beta_1) G'_i$$\n   \n   $$v_t = \\beta_2 v_{t-1} + (1-\\beta_2) (G'_i)^2$$\n   \n   $$\\hat{m}_t = \\frac{m_t}{1-\\beta_1^t}, \\quad \\hat{v}_t = \\frac{v_t}{1-\\beta_2^t}$$\n   \n   The standard Adam update is then applied in the rotated space:\n   \n   $$\\Delta W'_i = - \\eta \\frac{\\hat{m}_t}{\\sqrt{\\hat{v}_t} + \\epsilon}$$\n   \n   **Why does this work without explicit eigenvalue preconditioning?** The eigenvalues have already done their job by rotating us into the eigenbasis where the curvature is diagonal. In this space, Adam's assumption of independent directions becomes valid, so standard Adam works optimally.\n\n4. Rotate the update back to the original parameter space:\n   \n   $$\\Delta W_i = Q_R \\Delta W'_i Q_C^\\top$$\n\nIn short:  \nrotate the gradient into the eigenbasis (where directions are independent), apply Adam there, and rotate back.  \nThis aligns the update with the curvature, leading to more efficient steps.\n\n### Addressing Common Misconceptions\n\n**Misconception 1: \"KPOP is computationally less complex\"**\n\nThis is **wrong**! KPOP is actually **MORE expensive** per step than Adam:\n- Computing eigendecompositions: $O(d^3)$ for each covariance matrix\n- Matrix multiplications for rotations: $O(d^2)$ operations\n- Adam only needs element-wise operations: $O(d)$\n\n**Why use KPOP then?** Because it converges in **fewer steps**. Think of it like:\n- Adam: cheap car, long journey (many steps)\n- KPOP: expensive car, short journey (fewer steps)\n\n**Misconception 2: \"The space is reduced to 1D\"**\n\nThe space is **NOT** reduced to 1D! Here's what actually happens:\n\n- **Before rotation**: Optimization problem is coupled (changing one parameter affects how you should change others)\n- **After rotation**: Optimization becomes a collection of **independent** problems, each still in its own dimension\n- We still have the same number of parameters, just in a better coordinate system\n\n**Analogy:** Imagine you're solving a 2D maze, but the walls are diagonal. Instead of moving \"northeast\" and \"southeast\" (coupled directions), you rotate your view so you can move \"north\" and \"east\" independently. You're still in 2D, but navigation becomes much simpler.\n\n**Misconception 3: \"Only eigenvectors are used\"**\n\nBoth eigenvectors AND eigenvalues are crucial:\n- **Eigenvectors** ($Q_R, Q_C$): Define the rotation to independent coordinates\n- **Eigenvalues** ($\\Lambda_R, \\Lambda_C$): Define the natural coordinate system where the curvature is diagonal, making Adam's assumptions valid\n\nThe eigenvalues work *implicitly* by defining the coordinate transformation, not by explicit preconditioning of step sizes.\n\n## A Concrete Example: Understanding the Rotation\n\nLet's work through a simple $2\\times 2$ example to see exactly what \"rotation\" means.\n\n**Setup:** Imagine a layer with 2 inputs and 2 outputs, so the weight matrix $W$ is $2\\times 2$:\n\n$$W = \\begin{pmatrix} w_{11} & w_{12} \\\\ w_{21} & w_{22} \\end{pmatrix}$$\n\n**Step 1: Compute the gradient**\nSuppose our gradient is:\n\n$$G = \\begin{pmatrix} 1.0 & 0.5 \\\\ 0.5 & 1.0 \\end{pmatrix}$$\n\n**Step 2: Compute covariance matrices**\nFrom our batch, suppose we get:\n\n$$C = \\begin{pmatrix} 2.0 & 1.0 \\\\ 1.0 & 1.0 \\end{pmatrix}, \\quad R = \\begin{pmatrix} 1.5 & 0.5 \\\\ 0.5 & 0.5 \\end{pmatrix}$$\n\n**Step 3: Find eigenvectors (the \"rotation\" matrices)**\nFor $C$:\n\n$$C = Q_C \\Lambda_C Q_C^\\top = \\begin{pmatrix} 0.85 & -0.53 \\\\ 0.53 & 0.85 \\end{pmatrix} \\begin{pmatrix} 2.62 & 0 \\\\ 0 & 0.38 \\end{pmatrix} \\begin{pmatrix} 0.85 & 0.53 \\\\ -0.53 & 0.85 \\end{pmatrix}$$\n\nFor $R$:\n\n$$R = Q_R \\Lambda_R Q_R^\\top = \\begin{pmatrix} 0.92 & -0.39 \\\\ 0.39 & 0.92 \\end{pmatrix} \\begin{pmatrix} 1.82 & 0 \\\\ 0 & 0.18 \\end{pmatrix} \\begin{pmatrix} 0.92 & 0.39 \\\\ -0.39 & 0.92 \\end{pmatrix}$$\n\n**Step 4: \"Rotate\" the gradient**\n\n$$G' = Q_R^\\top G Q_C = \\begin{pmatrix} 0.92 & 0.39 \\\\ -0.39 & 0.92 \\end{pmatrix} \\begin{pmatrix} 1.0 & 0.5 \\\\ 0.5 & 1.0 \\end{pmatrix} \\begin{pmatrix} 0.85 & -0.53 \\\\ 0.53 & 0.85 \\end{pmatrix}$$\n\n$$G' = \\begin{pmatrix} 1.31 & 0.02 \\\\ 0.02 & 0.69 \\end{pmatrix}$$\n\n**What happened?**\n- **Original gradient $G$**: Had off-diagonal terms (0.5), meaning the gradient directions were coupled\n- **Rotated gradient $G'$**: Nearly diagonal! The off-diagonal terms (0.02) are almost zero\n\n**Why is this powerful?**\nIn the original coordinates, updating $w_{11}$ affects the loss in a way that depends on $w_{12}$, $w_{21}$, and $w_{22}$. Everything is tangled together.\n\nIn the rotated coordinates, each direction is nearly independent. We can optimize each direction separately without worrying about complex interactions.\n\n**Step 5: Apply Adam in the rotated space**\nNow we simply apply Adam to $G'$ where directions are independent:\n\n$$\\text{Adam\\_output} = \\begin{pmatrix} -0.131 & -0.002 \\\\ -0.002 & -0.069 \\end{pmatrix}$$\n\n**Why is this effective without explicit eigenvalue scaling?** \n\nThe magic happens because we're now in the eigenbasis! The eigenvalues have already \"rotated\" us into a coordinate system where:\n- Each direction is independent (nearly diagonal gradient)\n- Adam's assumptions are valid\n- The curvature information is implicitly encoded in the coordinate system itself\n\n**Step 6: Rotate back**\n\n$$\\Delta W = Q_R (\\text{Adam\\_output}) Q_C^\\top$$\n\n**What did we accomplish?**\n\n1. **Found natural coordinates**: The eigenvectors revealed the coordinate system where optimization directions are independent\n2. **Identified steepness**: The eigenvalues told us which directions are steep (high curvature) vs flat (low curvature)\n3. **Smart step sizing**: We took small steps in steep directions, big steps in flat directions\n4. **No more zig-zagging**: Instead of fighting against the problem's geometry, we aligned with it\n\n**The key insight:** KPOP doesn't reduce complexity - it **transforms** the problem into a form where each direction can be optimized independently with appropriate step sizes. This leads to faster convergence despite higher per-step cost.\n\n**Visual analogy:** Imagine you're trying to navigate in a city, but the streets are at weird angles. Instead of giving directions like \"go 3.2 blocks at 23В° angle,\" you:\n1. Rotate your map so the streets align with horizontal/vertical\n2. Give simple directions: \"go 2 blocks north, 4 blocks east\"\n3. Rotate back to the original map orientation\n\nThat's exactly what KPOP does with gradients!\n\n## Why Combine with Adam?\n\nAdam already adapts learning rates for each parameter based on past gradients, but it assumes the curvature is already diagonal вЂ” which is not true in the original space.  \nBy moving into the eigenbasis where curvature is diagonal, this assumption becomes true, making Adam more effective.\n\n## Pros and Cons of KPOP\n\n### Memory Overhead\n\nKPOP explicitly keeps and updates, for each layer $i$:\n- $R_i$ вЂ” output gradient covariance matrix ($d_{out}^{(i)} \\times d_{out}^{(i)}$)\n- $C_i$ вЂ” input activation covariance matrix ($d_{in}^{(i)} \\times d_{in}^{(i)}$)\n- $Q_{R,i}, \\Lambda_{R,i}$ вЂ” eigenvectors and eigenvalues of $R_i$\n- $Q_{C,i}, \\Lambda_{C,i}$ вЂ” eigenvectors and eigenvalues of $C_i$\n- $m_i, v_i$ вЂ” Adam's first and second moments ($d_{out}^{(i)} \\times d_{in}^{(i)}$)\n\nwhere $d_{out}^{(i)}$ and $d_{in}^{(i)}$ are the output and input dimensions of layer $i$, respectively.\n\nThis makes the memory requirement per layer:\n\n$$O\\big((d_{out}^{(i)})^2 + (d_{in}^{(i)})^2 + d_{out}^{(i)} \\cdot d_{in}^{(i)}\\big)$$\n\nBy contrast, Adam only keeps $m$ and $v$ ($d_{out}^{(i)} \\times d_{in}^{(i)}$) в†’ $O(d_{out}^{(i)} \\cdot d_{in}^{(i)})$.\n\nSo KPOP consumes more memory because it tracks the curvature approximation.\n\n### FLOPs per Progress\n\nOn the other hand, KPOP is more efficient per FLOP because:\n- The updates in the eigenbasis are better aligned with the true curvature.\n- Adam or SGD may require many more steps (and thus more FLOPs) to converge because they ignore curvature and may oscillate.\n- KPOP takes more informed steps, needing fewer of them to reach a good solution.\n\nEmpirically, wall-clock time and step count to reach a target loss are lower for KPOP than Adam, despite higher cost per step.\n\n### Hardware Considerations\n\nThis trade-off depends on hardware:\n- On NVIDIA H100, memory is scarce вЂ” so the $(d_{out}^{(i)})^2 + (d_{in}^{(i)})^2$ terms are expensive.\n- On Apple M3 Ultra, memory is abundant вЂ” so you can afford the extra curvature matrices and benefit from fewer FLOPs.\n\n**Concrete example:** Consider a transformer layer with $d_{in} = d_{out} = 4096$. \n- **Adam memory:** $2 \\times 4096^2 \\approx 32M$ parameters (for $m$ and $v$)\n- **KPOP memory:** $32M + 2 \\times 4096^2 \\approx 64M$ parameters (Adam + curvature matrices)\n\n**On H100 (80GB HBM):** Training a 70B parameter model is already tight on memory. The extra 32M parameters per layer $\\times$ 100 layers = 3.2B extra parameters might not fit.\n\n**On M3 Ultra (192GB unified memory):** The extra memory is easily affordable, and KPOP's faster convergence (requiring 30вЂ“50% fewer steps) often leads to better wall-clock time despite higher per-step cost.\n\n**The sweet spot:** KPOP shines when memory is available but compute time is the bottleneck вЂ” making it ideal for research settings, fine-tuning, or training on high-memory consumer hardware.\n\n## Conclusion\n\nKPOP is an elegant optimizer that:\n- Learns the curvature of the loss landscape.\n- Rotates into the natural axes of that landscape (eigenbasis).\n- Applies adaptive updates (like Adam) efficiently in that space.\n- Rotates back to the original parameters.\n\nThis allows it to move straight toward the minimum instead of zig-zagging вЂ” leading to faster, more robust training, even on consumer-grade hardware.\n\n**When should you use KPOP?**\n- You have sufficient memory (at least 2Г— what Adam needs)\n- Training time is more important than memory efficiency  \n- You're training on consumer hardware with abundant memory\n- You need robust convergence on complex loss landscapes\n\n**When to stick with Adam:**\n- Memory is extremely constrained (e.g., edge devices, large-scale distributed training)\n- Your model already converges quickly with simple optimizers\n- You're optimizing for minimal memory footprint over training speed\n\n**The big picture:** KPOP represents a fundamental shift from \"one-size-fits-all\" optimizers toward \"geometry-aware\" optimization. By understanding and adapting to the shape of the loss landscape, it achieves what mathematicians call \"quadratic convergence\" вЂ” the holy grail of optimization where each step becomes exponentially more effective.\n\nAs neural networks grow larger and more complex, this type of curvature-aware optimization may become essential for training the next generation of AI models efficiently.\n\n**Key ideas:**\n- Fisher matrix captures curvature.\n- KFAC approximates Fisher efficiently.\n- Eigenvectors/eigenvalues align us with independent directions.\n- Adam adapts step size for each direction.\n\nTogether, they make KPOP a powerful optimizer for large-scale training.\n\n## Further Reading\n\nIf you want to explore further:\n- Martens & Grosse (2015): KFAC\n- Kingma & Ba (2015): Adam\n- George et al.
